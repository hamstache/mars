MARS

History

  • Early Observations Before 1500
    The Babylonians developed advanced methods for predicting eclipses and future positions of Mars, but never attempted to explain what they witnessed.
    The Egyptians noticed the stars must be fixed and the sun moves relative to the stars. Planets of our Solar System were first observed (Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn).
    The Greeks named the planet Ares after their god of war. The Romans named the planet Mars after their god of war just like the Greeks. However, their respective cultures referred to the god of war by a different name.
  • Keen Eyesight 1500s
    Tycho Brahe (1546 - 1601), a Danish Astronomer, only had his eyesight to understand Mars and make calculations about its position that were accurate within four minutes of arc. He studied in his observatory on an island near Copenhagen for 20 years.
  • The First Telescopes 1600s
    1609 Galileo Galilei (1564 - 1642) becomes the first person to observe Mars with a primitive telescope for astronomical purposes.
    1659 Christiaan Huygens (1629 - 1695) draws Mars using his advanced telescope. His observations helped calculate that Mars had approximately a 24 hour day period.
    1666 Giovanni Cassini (1625 - 1712) observes Mars and concludes that the Mars day is exactly 24 hours and 40 minutes long.
    1672 Huygens notices signs of the souther polar cap at the south pole.
  • Growth of Telescopes 1700s
    1704 Giancomo Miraldi discovers that the southern cap is not centered on the rotational pole.
    1719 Mars moves in opposition and close to Earth; bringing panic to people due to the brightness of the sky.
    1727 In the text of Gulliver's Travels, Jonathan Swift describes Martian moons and their distances from Mars along with the time it takes for a full rotation.
    1777-1783 Sir William Herschel (1738 - 1822) studied Mars with telescopes he build himself. Believed there were inhabitants on Mars at one point in time with intelligent beings.
    1784 Hershcel concluded Mars had tenuous (weak) atmosphere, discussed the appearances of its polar regions, the inclination of its axis, the position of its poles, and spheroidal figure.
  • A Closer Look 1800s
    1809 Honore Flaugergues, French astronomer, notices dust clouds on the surface and refers to them as "yellow clouds."
    1862 Frederik Kaiser calculated the rotational period of Mars to be 24 hours, 37 minutes, 22.62 seconds, which is incredibly accurate to today's accepted value)
    1867 Richard Anthony Proctor publishes a map of Mars with continents and oceans.
    1867 Pierre Jules Janssen (1824 - 1907) and Sir William Huggins (1824 - 1910) make the first (unsuccessful) attempt to detect water vapor and oxygen spectroscopically (electromagnetic radiation).
    1877 Asaph Hall Discovers the moons of Mars. He names them Phobos (fear) and Deimos (freight) after the horses of the Greek war god, Ares (counterpart to the Roman war god, Mars).
  • Exploring the Surface 1900s to Present Day
    1964 Mariner 4 was the first spacecraft to fly by Mars and obtain close-up pictures of the Red Planet, passing within 9,844 kilometers of Mars.
    1969 Mariner 6 and 7 both succeeded in flybys of Mars at an altitude of 3,430 kilometers and returned 75 images from Mariner 6 and 126 images from Mariner 7.
    1971 Mariner 9 became the first spacecraft to go into orbit around another planet.
    1975 Viking 1 successfully orbited and eventualy landed on the surface of Mars. It recorded weather and conducted experiments on soil samples connected with a scoop.
    2003 Spirit and Opportunity landed on Mars and became one of the successful rover landings for NASA. Its extended mission allowed an encounter of evidence for past water. Mars Express also successfully entered orbit and began returning 3D, color images. It also detected methane and evidence for recent volcanism on Mars.
    2005 Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter is put into orbit of Mars to search for evidence of past water on Mars.
    2011 Curiosity rover builds on Spirit and Opportunity rover data. This includes detection of organic material.
    2013 MAVEN (Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution) provided measurements to address questions on climate and habitability.
