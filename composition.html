MARS

Composition

Taking a quick look at the surface of Mars it is hard to see much variation. Its surface displays an oxidized iron dust on top of similarly colored rock. However, this is not the entire story for the composition of this planet. We can take an even closer look to understand what exactly lies within this red, barren surface.

SURFACE COMPOSITION

The core of Mars is made up of sulfur, nickel, and iron. The radius of Mar's core measures between 900 to 1200 miles. The mantle of Mars is peridotite, which is a rock made up of primarily silicon, oxygen, iron, and magnesium. Olivine is the most abundant mineral in peridotite. Scientists do not know much more about the interior of Mars. The crust of Mars is made up of basalt. The dust that covers the surface is fine like talcum powder. The soil of Mars holds nutrients such as sodium, potassium, chloride, and magnesium. The crust is about 30 miles thick. Since Mars' crust is thought to be mostly one piece due to the fact that it has little to no tectonic movement, the molten rock flowed to the surface at the same point for successive eruptions that built up into huge volcanoes.

ATMOSPHERE COMPOSITION

The atmosphere of Mars is too thin to easily support life. It is 96% carbon dioxide, 1.93% Argon, 1.89% Nitrogen, 0.145% Oxygen, and less than 0.01% Carbon Monoxide. The air can be very dusty. This dust is often blown throughout the surface by giant dust devils. These dust storms are so vast in size that the red planet can be partly or wholly consumed by them.

MINEROLOGY

Mars is primarily an igneous planet. Therefore, it is important to know that minerals on the surface of Mars are mostly those that crystallize from magma. Data on the minerology of Mars has been collected through orbiting spacecrafts and rovers on the surface.

The Curiosity rover has a significant impact on the study of Mars' surface because it performed the first X-ray diffraction analysis of Martian soil. The results confirmed the presence of several minerals including feldspar, pyroxenes, and olivine.
